\section{Overview of the Presentation}

\paragraph{What.} We study the semantics of quantum programs extended with classically controlled quantum communication: sending and receiving qubits, possibly depending on a classical measurement outcome. For example, the following is naturally expressed in imperative syntax, but not in the standard quantum circuit model.
\begin{lstlisting}
  p: qbit = in(); q: qbit = new(|0>); 
  if (measure(p) == |1>) { out(q); }
\end{lstlisting}
The goal is to develop an axiomatisation of program equivalence between communicating quantum programs.

\paragraph{Why.} The broader research programme is to develop operationally and denotationally sound verification tools for distributed quantum programming~\cite{DQC_survey_Caleffi_2024}. Prior work focuses on quantum process calculi (QPCs)~\cite{qCCS_ying2010algebraquantumprocesses,CQP_gay2004communicatingquantumprocesses,QPAlg_lalire2004processalgebraicapproachconcurrent, lqCCS_10.1145/3632885}, which give a natural bisimulation-based verification technique for quantum protocols. QPCs are expressive (parallelism + communication + quantum effects) but complex and fundamentally operational. They lack a clear denotational reading compatible with quantum theory. Our strategy is to isolate quantum communication from concurrency, recovering a denotational reading alongside the operational one. Verification then happens through an equational axiomatisation.

\paragraph{How.} Concretely, we model communication operations as algebraic effects, following the tradition of Plotkin and Power~\cite{plotkin_power_2002}, as summarised by \cref{fig:triangle}. We propose a new algebraic theory for quantum communication building on \cite{statonpopl15} and study two models (operational and denotational). Finally we show the operational and denotational equalities coincide.

\section{Background: Axiomatising Quantum Computing}
We already know how to build the same triangle for qubit quantum computing without communication. Our starting point is \cite{statonpopl15}'s axiomatisation for qubit quantum computing, $\quantum$, based on \textit{parameterised} algebraic theories (PAT). PATs are a generic framework for axiomatising the behaviour of computations depending on certain resources in terms of operations and equations. Here, resources will correspond to qubits.

\paragraph{Operations} $\op: \arity{p}{m_1...m_k}$ represent computations which take $p$ qubits, classically choose one of the $k$ branches, say, $i$, and return $m_i$ qubits. $\quantum$ has three operations: state preparation $\new: (0 \mid 1)$, unitary evolution $\applyU: (n \mid n)$ for each $2^n \times 2^n$ unitary $U$, and computational basis measurement \& classical control $\opmeasure: (1 \mid 0, 0)$.

\paragraph{Terms} generated from the operations follow the same intuition: a term $\typing{x_1: m_1...x_k: m_k}{q_1...q_p}{c}$ takes $p$ input qubits $q_1...q_p$ and calls one of the $k$ continuations $x_1...x_k$ with the corresponding number of qubits. We can thus write for example the program 
  \[
  \typing{x: 1, y: 1}{q_1, q_2}{\opmeasure(q_1, x(q_2), y(q_2))}
  \]
  which measures $q_1$ and continues with $x$ or $y$ with $q_2$ based on the measurement outcome. 
\paragraph{Equations} are given in the form $\typing{\Gamma}{\Delta}{c=c'}$. $\quantum$ for example specifies the effect of measuring a $\ket|0>$ qubit using the following equation:
\[
\typing{x: 0, y: 0}{\cdot}{\new(q. \opmeasure(q, x, y)) = x}
\]

\paragraph{Models: Denotational and Operational} \cite{statonpopl15} proceeds to define a denotational model for the $\quantum$ theory, where each term $\typing{x_1:m_1...x_k:m_k}{q_1...q_p}{c}$ is interpreted as a complete positive unital (CPU) map $$\sem{c}: \bigoplus_{i=1}^k\Mat_{2^{m_i}}(\complex) \to \Mat_{2^p}(\complex).$$ Remarkably, this model is \emph{fully complete}: every CPU map of this type is the interpretation of some term $c$, and two CPU maps are equal iff their term representations are derivably equal in the equational theory. 

We can also give an equivalent operational model for $\quantum$. Briefly, configurations are of the form $\langle \rho, c \rangle$ where $\rho$ is the program state, $c$ the running program, and transitions $\to_p$ are probabilistic. We can then formulate in terms of $\to_p$ an operational notion of program equivalence $\quequiv$ equivalent to denotational equality. 

\section{Semantics for Quantum Communication}
We proceed to extend our language with communication primitives, and strive to recover the same triangle. 

\subsection{An Algebraic Theory for Quantum Communication}
Extending $\quantum$ with two operations: $\opin: (0 \mid 1)$ and $\opout: (1 \mid 0)$ lets us represent classically controlled communication. We can express the example program as $\typing{x: 0}{\cdot}{\opin(p. \new(q. \opmeasure(p, x, \opout(q, x))))}$. We further add equations to make $\opin$ and $\opout$ commute with every $\quantum$ operation. What we now obtain is $\iotq$, where $\otimes$ denotes the commutative combination of two theories. This choice is motivated by quantum theory. For instance,  
$$\opmeasure(a,\; \opout(b, x),\; \opout(b, y)) = \opout(b,\; \opmeasure(a, x, y))$$
is required to derive the equivalence between: preparing a Bell pair, sending one qubit and discarding the other; and tossing a fair coin and sending the outcome as a qubit. 

\subsection{An Operational Model}
Concretely, the notion of communication that our theory soundly axiomatises can be demonstrated through an operational model. We work in $\CP$, the category of finite dimensional C* algebras and complete positive maps, constructed as in \cite{selinger_dagger_compact_closed_cat_cp_maps}. 

The operational semantics is based on a \emph{quantum stream}, specifying the environment our program communicates with. The idea is to have a global quantum state $\rho: \complex \to S \otimes \qbit \otimes H$, where $S$ is the program state, $H$ is the hidden stream state, and $\qbit$ holds the next input qubit. Then, a stream is a state machine reacting to the program's communications: formally, a stream $t$ is specified by a hidden state type $H_\sigma$ and transition maps $T_{\opin}(\sigma)$, $T_{\opout}(\sigma)$ for every communication trace $\sigma \in \traceset$. 

We can then build an operational semantics with configuration $\config{\rho}{\sigma}{c}$, where $\rho$ is the global state, $\sigma \in \traceset$ is the communication trace, and $c$ is the program. Given a stream specification $t$, we can give a small-step transition relation $\to^t_p$ defined inductively on the program. For example, for the output operation, the rule is as in \cref{fig:opsemout}: the output is performed, and the stream state is updated according to $t$. Finally, $\quequiv$ extends naturally to communicating quantum programs and gives a sound model of $\iotq$. 
\begin{theorem}
  \label{thm:soundopsem}
  $\typing{\Gamma}{\Delta}{c=c'} \implies \typing{\Gamma}{\Delta}{c \quequiv c'}$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{A Denotational Model}
\paragraph{The Quantum I/O Monad} In the tradition of algebraic effects \cite{plotkin_power_2002,sums_tensors_HYLAND200670}, we build an I/O monad ($T(X) = \mu Y. [\qbitin, Y] \oplus \qbitout \otimes Y \oplus X$, where the $\opin$ and $\opout$ superscripts are bookkeeping) directly on top of $\CP$. On the one hand, we can simplify the definition of $T$ by unrolling the fixed point and by noting that $[\qbitin, Y] \cong \qbitin \otimes Y$ by compact closure and self-duality: 
\[
T (X) = \bigoplus_{\sigma \in \traceset} (\qbit^{\sigma} \otimes X) 
\]
where $\qbit^{s_1...s_n} = \qbit^{s_1} \otimes \dots \otimes \qbit^{s_n}$, $s_i \in \{\opin, \opout\}$. On the other hand, $T$ is not well-defined because $\CP$ only has finite biproducts. To make it well-defined, we follow \cite{PaganiSV13} and complete $\CP$ with infinite biproducts using a dcpo completion, obtaining $\CPinf$. Now $T$ is well-defined as a functor $\CPinf \to \CPinf$, which is in fact a monad. 

% This definition makes intuitive sense: the I/O trace of a computation determines exactly how many input qubits it will depend on, and how many output qubits it will generate. 

\paragraph{Denotational Semantics} We simply interpret each $\typing{\Gamma}{\Delta}{c}$ term as a morphism $\sem{c}: \sem{\Delta} \to T(\sem{\Gamma})$ in the Kleisli category of $T$. The interesting case is the input operator $\sem{\opin}: \complex \to T(\qbit)$, defined as 
\[
   \sem{\opin} \eqdef \inj^{\opin} \circ \left[\tikzfig{monadic_sem_in}\right] 
\]
where the `cup' is the unnormalised Bell state. Intuitively, $\sem{\opin}$ is supposed to take a new input; the cup bends the input wire up so it appears on the output side, as specified by the monad. Remarkably, this denotation corresponds exactly to the operational notion of equality, $\quequiv$. We thus get: 
\begin{theorem}
  \label{thm:adeqfullabs}
  $\typing{\Gamma}{\Delta}{c \quequiv c'} \iff \sem{c} = \sem{c'}$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{corollary}
  \label{thm:sounddensem}
  $\typing{\Gamma}{\Delta}{c=c'} \implies \sem{c} = \sem{c'}$.
\end{corollary}

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

We have obtained a sound axiomatisation $\iotq$ for one notion of quantum communication, presented in two equivalent ways: operationally and denotationally -- a first step towards equational verification of quantum protocols. However, unlike the quantum computing case, we do not have completeness -- whether $\sem{c}=\sem{c'} \implies \typingfilled{c = c'}$ is open. Achieving this is our immediate next step. Beyond that, we are interested in connecting this work back to quantum process calculi (e.g.~\cite{lqCCS_10.1145/3632885}) and higher-order quantum computing \cite{PaganiSV13,kissinger2019categorical}.
